- Minnow had never died before. But this was far from their first encounter with death. The deaths of loved ones. Deaths of innocents and non-innocents alike by their own hand. The slaying of monsters humanoid and beastly.
  This type of death though— their own— was a novel experience that they found themselves not hoping to repeat. Although not for the reasons one might think: Minnow had no qualms with the way death came about them, nor with the pain associated, or the many points of unfinished business Minnow had left to attend to. Minnow could come to terms with all of this, and was actually quite pleased with the ‘how it happened’ of it all. A deep and unyielding sense of Pride had hammered itself painstakingly into Minnow’s bones the moment the Angel of Wrath had set its sights on taking them down. To say Minnow was pleased with themselves that they had put up such a fight and were still struck down by an angel of their God, was a bit of hubris that still may be understating it.
  And pain was no stranger to them. Pain was part and parcel to Vengeance and Wrath.
  As for the unfinished business: Minnow had friends that would take care of their menial tasks and keep people safe. And Minnow trusted them, more than perhaps anything else.
  The bit of death that bothered Minnow was the Afterlife. The continuing on. The dragging echoes of breathing and blood-pumping that clung to them even after their body could no longer undertake the acts.
  As soon as Minnow’s body stopped breathing— the battle still raging around them (the arms of an angel galaxied above them, the screams of soldiers and civilians fading to nothing, the muted humming of Bladesong) and their final vestiges of Wrath left them— they caught a glimpse of nothing.
  Blissful nothing. Silence. Not an echo. Not a thought.
  And it was only in that moment that Minnow knew death. For in the next, Minnow was Cento again.
  Cento with his mother and father and sister. In a beautiful stone house beside a swift, clear river. Verdant grass and rich, dark soil. A sky so blue and clear it was nearly white with the sun. He breathed the scent of crisp autumn air and the petrichoric wash of the river. It mingled pleasantly with the waft of fresh fish and turned earth from his side. A fishing pole had been dug into the ground and held up by stones pushed against. Next to it sat a woven basket of salmon, trout, crayfish, and minnows— still flopping and crawling weakly.
  Cento’s freshest catch would fetch high prices at market, and whatever he couldn’t sell would feed his family that night. Nothing brought Cento more satisfaction
  or joy than seeing his sister, Wanaea, grin with her whole face. Or watching his mother’s hands, flashing with gold rings and glittering bracelets, flutter over mismatched silverware as she delicately cuts into the flaky pink meat of the bounty Cento has brought home. Or hearing his father come home from a long day of work, sloughing off his heavy coat and boots, to enter a kitchen full of laughter and joy.
  Not a day went by where Cento didn’t think this was a dream. As though a bit of cold breeze might bring in the winter and with it the death of all this beautiful life. A family full of life and that wanted for nothing because of his efforts. The food and income he could provide. He had made this— moulded this life into existence from the mucked silt at the bottom of the river.
  Cento gathered his catch and his fishing pole, thoughts of returning home to a warm hearth, a fragrant kitchen, and the contentment of his family drawing him away from the toil of labor. Towards a life he would grasp onto greedily for as long as he could.
  Minnow was a wanderer— stuck but eternally moving. A field of dry, cracking, pale grasses stretches before them. An intertwining venous web of silvery streams glimmers in between the rolling of grasses in the wind. Minnow kneels on the stream bank of a meandered curve. The water’s flow is gentle and the stream is hardly wider than Minnow’s wrist. Beneath the steady rush of crystal clear water, pebbles tumble over each other and roil along the stream.
  Minnow extracts a hand from its heavy gauntlet. Their hand is trembling, and steam wafts off it in the frigid air. Their skin is snow pale and pruned like well-worn paper. Minnow reaches their hand towards the stream and lets the ice cold water run through their fingers. No minnows, no shells, nothing worthwhile. Minnow, practiced, retracts and flicks the water from their hand. Just another futile attempt to catch something that has never been within their reach. But Minnow would keep walking along the rivers— even though their desires were out of their reach, Minnow would keep outstretching their hand.
  They stand. Their knees crackle and their face burns from the biting air, but warmth still puffs out from their breaths. The overcast sky casts meek, diffused light across a landscape unbroken by buildings or trees. No mountains line the horizon, no hills vary the long walk ahead. Minnow turns to scan the path behind them, the land they’ve already traversed. It all looks the same. They turn back quickly. It wouldn’t do to get lost and backtrack; it’s best to remember the way forward— to move further, not behind.
  Minnow hasn’t found anything to ear, or kindling for fire, or shelter to sleep under in… they’re actually not sure how long it’s been. Not how far they have left to go. Or what their goal is. All they know is that they should keep traveling. Because if they stop, then there’s nothing left. They’ll never find the end of a stream, or any minnows, or any warmth, contentment, rest.
  But they will find it. Whatever ‘it’ may be. They will.
  Minnow has failed. Killed an Angel of Wrath. Disarmed it of its limbs and of its weapons. Hacked into its mountainous body, sinew and lean muscle snapping under their glaive. Ichor spilling onto cobbles, so abundant it fills the broken fountain in the square. Minnow’s body heaves, sweat drips into their eyes, mingling with blood and grime. They reach up and yank the cracked visage of a barn owl from their face. The mask falls from their fingertips and clatters to the ground, lying in pieces among the glimmering red of god-blood. Cento’s face looks back at Minnow for the first time in an age. The scars wrought by their own hand mar the planes of their cheeks and caress their lips. They are ugly, torn through skin and badly healed. Cento’s eyes cannot open fully due to the scar tissue around their sockets, and the hair of their eyebrows grows unevenly in patches. Their eyes themselves are dark, black as though bottled night was poured in, only the white sclera providing any gleam.
  Minnow tends to avoid their reflection, but in the blood of an angel, they seem unable to look away. Their friends have fallen, slain by Wrath incarnate. Minnow unable to save them. Only able to use their Wrath to kill.
  There was no honor in that. Their companions die, and they kill an angel sent by their Patron? The reason they’re able to seek vengeance at all? None survive but Minnow themselves. What could be more greedy, more corrupt than this?
  Minnow looks to the bodies of Ottenean soldiers, the bodies of Ampelia, Vitka, Derdu, Robert, Saph, Polly, and Thraern. The people they were protecting from Greed, and from the demons they might send. From the cohort of taking and hoarding.
  But now Minnow has taken more than Greed ever could. Minnow has taken their oath and squandered it. Has taken their friends’ trust and wasted it. What use was Vengeance if they did nothing but forfeit lives in pursuit? If they fought an Angel of Wrath rather than obey it?
  Minnow falls. Their knees sting against the hard stone beneath them, and their palms scrape along gravel coated thickly in blood. Minnow cups their hands to scoop up as much blood as they can. A small pool forms in their palms. Ripples marble across the surface, as though something moves within.
  To the surface, a pearly iridescent fish, no larger than Minnow’s little finger, floats. Dead-eyed and belly up. Everything they’ve sought within the palms of their hands.
  Minnow loosens the pressure of their fingers and allows the blood to seep through. Once the fish is all that remains, they dip their head and take it between their teeth. The pop of its flesh and crunch of its bones fills their mouth. The taste of rotted fish is putrid and coats their tongue, but the satisfaction it brings is a worthwhile reward.
  Minnow cups their hands and collects wrath’s blood once more; they bring it to their lips to drink of it. The red drips down their chin and neck, coating them. It is warm. And it warms them to the core. It is heady, and greed feels good.
  A broken oath is freeing, and Minnow is more wrathful than ever.
