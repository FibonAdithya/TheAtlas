- The Duat is a tranquil sea that exists beneath the chaos of the other planes. To exist here is to be definitionally dreaming, and to experience all the oddities that come with that. Lack of short term memory, and inability to discern detail, or even your own motives for acting, to name a few.
- Arriving in the Duat, one would see a great black sea of shimmering oil, set beneath a brilliant sky of many peacefully burning suns. The suns wink occasionally, as if the sky itself closed its eyes. Though the suns do not light up the Duat effectively, they do provide power for the solar-sail vessels which coast the fumes from the oil sea.
- There are pillars which rise from the seabed to the sky, and their tops are often wreathed in storms. Occasionally these towers lean together, like trees in the wind, and their storms may clash or drift or react to one another. Even rarer, the towers may collapse into the oil sea, setting free their storms to roam the Duat. There are settlements built into these towers. It is thought that these towers represent links from other realms to here - a sort of ladder back up to your world. But the stone is smooth, and they are hard to climb.
- Here's a small primer bit. I'll write this as from the perspective of Elveszett ('lost' in Hungarian), a soldier from the deserts far north of Kingarrock. She fights marauders that harry the outskirts of Halicarnassus, and her company has chased some particularly driven ones into a cave system. Here, they encounter a strange blue glow, and continue the fight into a silver forest. They fight on but begin to be overwhelmed by the marauders. A handful of birdlike humanoids emerge, with sleek plumage and jewellery adorning their beaks and claws (Proudplume family of shrykes). Quickly, the company is decimated. The few survivors include Elveszett, and the next thing she knows the brambles in the forest move to entangle them, sending them to sleep. The others merely dream but Elveszett cannot dream, never has, and so falls deeper - dreams into the Duat. Cue a description of what she sees: the sea of oil, with plumes of gas and roving patches of multicoloured fire. The solar ships coasting these fumes by the light of a whole sky of blinking orange suns, against a backdrop of cosmic beauty. The towers that rise from the inky depths, swaying in an unfelt wind, with lightning storms at their summits many miles up. The bones, sand, and glass that form beaches at the base of many towers. The laughter and chatter heard from the village carved into the tower she sits beneath.
	- Five riders flank you in V-formation. Oxen steeds kick dust as they are spurred from the city limits, out into the desert. The quarry you seek hides across the basin. They languish in pothole caves beneath a boulder field, like a nest. Before long, the company is upon them in the scree.
	  
	  Fierce fight the marauders, but they've no training to match that from your Crown City. They get one of the company, a shame. Adapt formation, move into the cave entrance. How are there so many of them? Why do they fight like fanatics? These answers are demanded direct by the Sovereign. You are Elveszett, the one who will retrieve them.
	  
	  Deeper into the cave than thought possible the company has found a silver forest. A forest, beneath the desert. For the canopy you cannot see the stone roof. And there are creatures leading the marauders occasionally. Birdlike things, that your myths call Hentesmadár. Jewellery and slicked back, colourful plumage around wicked beaks and claws that hold the weapons of slavers. Whips and flails, bludgeons and clubs - weapons for punching down, not equally. And they have magic too. The first one you found sent a soldier to sleep, and it was as good as dead from there. Now your remaining three men die to sheer numbers and your hope runs out.
	  
	  Your species cannot sleep, don't need to - but as the hentesmádar reaches out for you it does anyway, such is its power in this place. Your mind has never dreamed, Elveszett, and cannot find thoughts or memories to cling to. So you fall through and out of consciousness, and find yourself in a strange place indeed.
	  
	  Stupor recedes from your mind, but your memories are not left intact. You do not hear the crunch of bones beneath your feet, or the sound of waves encircling a shore, for the sights that stun your eyes. You are looking at an ocean. But it is not of blue water - it is a sea of black pitch, and its waves are thickly rolling as such, bubbling and gouting gasses. In places this ocean is on fire, like orange grass on a black dune. 
	  
	  At the horizon far away, this tar sea meets a sky equally as strange. It is lit by a thousand amber suns, but their light seems not to reach the place where you are. Beyond these suns you can observe nebulae, columns of light and technicolour clouds of scale so awesome as to be incomprehensible. There are regions of absolute absence, where no stars are, not even the soft close amber suns. Looking at these regions makes you shudder. 
	  
	  Then there are pillars, like huge stone tree trunks, that hold the heavens aloft. They must be one hundred metres across, and miles high - higher than your eyes can see detail. Each is different stone, or a new material entirely. The closest to you is perhaps five hundred metres across the sea and made of silver. It appears to have branches and foliage coming from it, which sprout glowing blue flowers. Nearby that one, you see a black tower which seems to shower ash into the ocean, like falling leaves. They sway in a wind that you cannot feel on your skin. The forest of pillars is sparse, not dense. You can see fragments of unknown material drift the waves, and to confirm your suspicion you hear an almighty crash far off. One column has fallen, shattering as it collapses, kicking up ugly waves from the sea. These fragments must be pillar driftwood. You wonder how rare an occasion this is. You wonder if there exists an equal process by which these trees originate.
	  
	  There are lights on these towers. Some are close to the ground, and some are miles up. From behind you, you can hear the faintest sounds of conversation, and laughter. You turn, and see you are at the base of the pillar, where it meets the sea. A small shore made of sediment, rounded glass, but predominantly bones in various states of sea-smoothing, is saving you from contact with the oil. A hundred metres above you, buildings twinkle light from their windows, and walkways show tiny figures walk across them chatting to each other. You think you see a cafe, or maybe a restaurant. Someone almost trips and falls but catches themself. They laugh nervously with a friend but you cannot hear what they say. 
	  
	  Your pillar is made of iron. Roughly hammered and with imperfections and cracks, but in the form of a great tree. For some reason this feels the natural shape for the iron to take. There are some pitons and oil lamps forced into the cracks. Atop yours, and the other pillars, are great storms of fire and lightning which hurt to look at. When sway the pillars, so sway these storms, and collide with one another in bright flashes. Some are untethered, and several free tempests drift the skies. Unlike any of the distant pillars you can see, your iron tree splits into a gnarl of branches at its summit, and is crowned by the greatest storm of all those you can see. It is as if the greatest lightning bolt from this storm has struck the ocean, and frozen into iron right at this moment. 
	  
	  Then Elveszett your eyes see a great ship hurtle through the air above you - it is like the ships you have seen when once you ventured to the coast in your world. A great wooden hull, sleek and sporting many decks. Atop it three masts, billowing golden gossamer sails that shimmer and glow and the light of the amber suns seems to give impetus to the vessel. The main mast continues beneath the hull, allowing a fourth set of sails to add power from under the keel. As it passes closest by you, you can see the words 'Ad Somnium Profundum' in silver filigree on its hull - the figurehead of something coiled and many-armed and marine-looking on its prow. The speed it takes is blinding, and in seconds it is gone into the night.
	  
	  You look out into this great vault, the oil sea and the burning sky with strange pillar trees supporting. You know that doom lurks in those black regions of space and horror beneath the waves, but that the amber suns are kind and that the people in these villages are alive. As in a dream, you cannot remember how you got here. You begin to forget that your name was ever Elveszett, and do not even wonder or panic at how you might return. Do you even want to?
